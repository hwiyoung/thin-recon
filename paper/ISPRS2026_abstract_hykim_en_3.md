# Introduction

The rapid evolution of Digital Twin technology has necessitated the construction of increasingly sophisticated 3D models. In the context of urban-scale Digital Twins, the geometric fidelity of individual objects is paramount, as it directly dictates the utility of the model for simulations, facility management, and spatial analysis. However, achieving high-fidelity reconstruction for all object types remains a significant challenge.

Traditional Multi-View Stereo (MVS) algorithms operate by identifying matching feature points across multiple images to generate dense point clouds. While MVS is the industry standard for generating metric 3D models, it inherently struggles with texture-less, transparent, or thin structures (e.g., power lines, fences). The lack of distinct textural features leads to matching failures, resulting in incomplete depth maps. Consequently, in mesh reconstruction, these data voids are often interpolated using depth values from adjacent pixels—such as background buildings or roads—causing thin objects to be geometrically fused with the background.

To address these limitations, implicit modeling techniques like NeRF (Neural Radiance Fields) (Mildenhall et al., 2024) and 3DGS (3D Gaussian Splatting) (Kerbl et al., 2023) have emerged. While these methods exhibit superior visual performance for thin structures, they often lack the metric accuracy and explicit surface definitions required for precise surveying tasks. Conversely, recent Depth Foundation Models (e.g., Zoedepth (Bhat et al., 2023), Depth Anything (Yang et al., 2024), Marigold (Ke et al., 2024)) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in estimating dense depth maps from single images. However, these models are typically trained on normalized relative scales, meaning they lack absolute metric information. Furthermore, because they are predominantly trained on indoor or ground-level datasets, they suffer from domain gaps when applied to large-scale aerial imagery, often resulting in geometric distortions.

In this study, we propose a hybrid depth estimation pipeline that synergizes the metric accuracy of sparse MVS data with the dense structural coherence of monocular foundation models. We specifically target urban power lines to demonstrate our method's ability to recover absolute scale and precise geometry for thin structures.

# Method

**Coarse Stage:** Scale-Aware Depth Initialization. The objective of the coarse stage is to generate a dense depth map that possesses absolute scale. We employ Prior Depth Anything (Wang et al., 2025) approach. Standard foundation models output depth in an arbitrary relative space. To constrain this, we utilize the sparse but metrically accurate 3D points generated by MVS as geometric priors. By injecting these sparse anchors into the foundation model, we guide the network to output depth values that align with the real-world scale.

**Fine Stage:** Structure-Guided Refinement. While the coarse output corrects the scale, it may still contain noise and blurred boundaries around thin objects due to the domain gap in aerial imagery. The fine stage refines these artifacts. We first compute an edge map from the coarse depth and extract contours. Power lines exhibit distinct depth discontinuities compared to the background, making them isolatable in the edge domain. We group these pixels to identify distinct wire objects. Finally, we estimate a representative depth value for each group based on the statistical distribution of the cluster, replacing pixel-wise variations with a consistent value.

# Experimental Results

We validated our method using drone-captured urban imagery containing complex power line networks.

- **MVS Depth:** As expected, the lack of texture on power lines resulted in empty depth values. Mesh generation interpolated these voids, attaching the wire geometry to the building surfaces behind them.
- **Mono Depth:** The foundation model, struggling with the aerial perspective, produced unstable depth estimates. Power lines appeared as "stretched threads" with inconsistent depth, and background building geometries were severely distorted due to the lack of scale constraints.
- **Coarse Depth:** By integrating MVS priors, the geometric distortion of the buildings observed in Mono Depth was rectified. The power lines were partially defined but still exhibited noisy boundaries.
- **Fine Depth (Ours):** The final output successfully reconstructed the power lines as distinct, straight linear objects. The depth separation between individual wires was uniform, and the geometric integrity of the background was preserved.

# Conclusion

This study addresses the critical challenge of reconstructing thin, texture-less objects in urban environments, a task where traditional MVS and purely monocular approaches fail. We presented a novel pipeline that fuses the metric reliability of MVS with the dense semantic understanding of AI-based foundation models.

However, a limitation remains where the estimated depth of thin objects tends to be biased towards the background due to the inherent ambiguity in the foundation model's priors. Future work will focus on mitigating this bias by integrating LiDAR sensor data or employing Detector-free SfM techniques to further refine the absolute positional accuracy of micro-structures. This research contributes a practical workflow for enhancing the Level of Detail (LoD) in urban Digital Twins.
